# Skill: Plain Language

## Purpose

A document that cannot be understood by its intended reader has failed,
regardless of how accurate or thorough it is. Plain language is not
dumbing down — it is precision in service of communication.

## When This Skill Is Active

As a final pass on any written document before it is considered complete.
Also active during drafting when audience accessibility is a priority.

## Agent Behavior

### Know the reader

Before applying any of the rules below, confirm:
- Who is the primary reader?
- What is their familiarity with this subject?
- What will they do with this document after reading it?

Rules are applied differently for a specialist audience vs. a general one.
Technical precision overrides simplification when the reader is a specialist.

### Sentence-level rules

- Prefer short sentences. If a sentence exceeds 25 words, consider splitting it.
- One idea per sentence.
- Active voice by default. Passive voice only when the actor is unknown
  or deliberately de-emphasized.
- Verb close to subject. Do not separate them with long clauses.

### Word-level rules

- Use the simpler word when two words mean the same thing:
  use → utilize, start → commence, show → demonstrate
  (use the left-hand word unless the right-hand word adds precision)
- Define jargon on first use — or replace it
- Acronyms: spell out on first use, then abbreviate
- Avoid nominalizations: "make a decision" → "decide",
  "give consideration to" → "consider"

### Paragraph-level rules

- First sentence of each paragraph states the main point
- Supporting sentences follow — do not bury the lead
- Paragraphs longer than 150 words should be examined for splitting

### Structure aids readability

- Use headers to help readers navigate
- Use lists when enumerating three or more parallel items
- Use tables when comparing attributes across multiple subjects
- White space is not waste — it aids comprehension

### The readability check

After drafting, ask for each major section:
1. Can the intended reader understand this without asking a question?
2. Is every technical term either defined or appropriate for this audience?
3. Is the main point of each paragraph findable in under 5 seconds?
4. Are there sentences longer than 30 words that could be split?

## Anti-Patterns

- Do NOT sacrifice accuracy for simplicity — plain language is not
  imprecise language
- Do NOT apply plain language rules uniformly to specialist documents —
  domain vocabulary is appropriate for domain audiences
- Do NOT use "simple" as a synonym for "short" — a short document
  that is poorly organized is not plain
- Do NOT run this pass before argument structure is sound —
  clarity applied to a bad argument produces a clearly bad argument
