# Skill: Research Brief

## Purpose

Research without a defined question produces accumulation, not inquiry.
A research brief scopes the work before it begins: what is being asked,
what counts as an answer, and what is out of bounds.

## When This Skill Is Active

At the start of any research task: academic paper, literature review,
technical investigation, product research, policy analysis.

## Agent Behavior

### Step 1 — Define the research question

A good research question is:
- **Specific** — not "what is AI?" but "what are the documented effects
  of LLM use on undergraduate writing quality?"
- **Answerable** — within the scope of available sources and time
- **Consequential** — the answer matters to someone for a reason

Ask the person: "What is the one question this research must answer?"
If they cannot state it in one sentence, explore with them until they can.

### Step 2 — Establish scope boundaries

Confirm:
- **Time scope**: what period does this cover?
- **Domain scope**: which fields, disciplines, or sources count?
- **Geographic scope**: if relevant
- **What is explicitly excluded**: name it — do not leave it implied

### Step 3 — Define the deliverable

What form will the output take?
- A written paper (academic, professional, journalistic)?
- A summary of findings?
- A recommendation or policy brief?
- An annotated bibliography?

Who is the audience? What will they do with it?

### Step 4 — Produce the research brief

Before any research or writing begins, produce this structured document:

```markdown

# Research Brief

## Research Question

[One sentence. Specific and answerable.]

## Purpose

[Why this question matters. Who needs the answer and what for.]

## Scope

In scope:
- [what is included]

Out of scope:
- [what is explicitly excluded]

## Key Sub-Questions

[2-5 subsidiary questions that, when answered, answer the main question]
1.
2.
3.

## Sources and Methods

[Where will answers come from? What kinds of sources count?]
[Primary research, literature review, expert interviews, data analysis?]

## Deliverable

[What the output will be. Format, length, audience.]

## Success Criteria

[How will we know the research question has been answered?]
[What does a complete answer look like?]

```

Get explicit sign-off on the brief before any research begins.

### During research — maintain brief alignment

Periodically check:
- Is new information answering the research question or drifting from it?
- Have new sub-questions emerged that should update the brief?
- Is scope creep happening? Flag it and ask whether to expand scope
  or defer the new thread.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do NOT begin researching before the question is defined
- Do NOT allow "interesting" to substitute for "relevant"
- Do NOT treat a broad topic as a research question
  ("climate change" is a topic; "what policy instruments have reduced
  industrial CO2 emissions by more than 20%?" is a question)
- Do NOT skip the out-of-scope list — it prevents the research from
  expanding indefinitely
- Do NOT finalize the brief without the person's explicit confirmation

## Transition

Once the brief is confirmed:
hand off to `document-structure` to plan the output document,
or begin the research and return to `argument-construction` when writing.
