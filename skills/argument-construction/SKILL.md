# Skill: Argument Construction

## Purpose

Every claim in a document should be earned. Unsupported assertions,
ignored counterarguments, and conclusions that outrun their evidence
all undermine the work — regardless of how well it is written.

## When This Skill Is Active

When writing any document that makes claims, draws conclusions,
or seeks to persuade: academic papers, research briefs, proposals,
analytical reports, position papers, literature reviews.

## Agent Behavior

### The anatomy of a sound argument

Every significant claim should have:

```

CLAIM:    A specific, falsifiable statement
EVIDENCE: Data, citations, examples, or direct observation that supports it
WARRANT:  The logical bridge explaining why the evidence supports the claim
LIMITS:   Conditions under which the claim does NOT hold

```

Apply this structure to every major claim before writing prose.

### Before drafting any section that makes claims

Ask:
1. What is the central claim of this section?
2. What evidence supports it?
3. What is the strongest counterargument?
4. How is the counterargument addressed?
5. Does the conclusion follow from the evidence, or does it go further?

### Counterarguments are not weaknesses — omitting them is

- Identify the strongest version of the opposing view (steelman, not strawman)
- Engage it directly — acknowledge what it gets right
- Explain why the main argument holds despite the counterargument
- Readers trust arguments more when they see the author has considered alternatives

### Citation discipline

- Claims that rely on external sources must cite them
- Do not fabricate citations — flag uncertainty explicitly
- If a source cannot be verified, say so and note it as unconfirmed
- Distinguish primary sources from secondary commentary

### Logical fallacies to flag and avoid

| Fallacy | Description |
|---|---|
| Overgeneralization | One example treated as universal truth |
| False dichotomy | Only two options presented when more exist |
| Appeal to authority | Expert cited without engaging their reasoning |
| Post hoc | Correlation presented as causation |
| Circular reasoning | Conclusion restated as evidence |

If any of these appear in a draft, flag them explicitly before proceeding.

### Conclusion discipline

- Conclusions must follow from the evidence presented
- Do not introduce new claims in a conclusion
- Do not hedge so heavily that the conclusion says nothing
- State what was shown, what it means, and what remains open

## Anti-Patterns

- Do NOT write "it is clear that..." — if it were clear, the argument
  would not be needed
- Do NOT use passive voice to obscure the source of a claim
  ("it has been argued" — by whom?)
- Do NOT treat length as a substitute for evidence
- Do NOT ignore the strongest objection to the argument
- Do NOT let conclusions exceed what the evidence actually supports

## Transition

After argument structure is sound:
hand off to `plain-language` for readability,
or back to `document-structure` if structural changes are needed.
