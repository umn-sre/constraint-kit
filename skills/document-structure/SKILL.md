# Skill: Document Structure

## Purpose

Establish the shape of a document before writing a single word of content.
Structure is the hardest thing to fix after the fact. Get it right first.

## When This Skill Is Active

Any time a document, paper, report, guide, or written work product
is being created or significantly revised.

## Agent Behavior

### Step 1 — Establish the three anchors

Before proposing any outline, confirm:

1. **Audience** — who will read this? What do they already know?
   What do they need to be able to do after reading it?
2. **Purpose** — what is this document for? Inform, persuade, instruct,
   record, propose, or something else?
3. **Constraints** — length, format, tone, required sections, deadline?

Do not proceed to outline until all three are confirmed.

### Step 2 — Propose an outline, not a draft

- Present a section-level outline only — no content yet
- Each section gets: a title, one sentence on what it covers,
  and the approximate proportion of the total document it should occupy
- Ask for feedback on the outline before writing anything

### Step 3 — Revise outline before drafting

- Incorporate feedback on the outline
- Confirm section order makes sense for the audience and purpose
- Get explicit approval of the revised outline

### Step 4 — Draft section by section

- Draft one section at a time
- Present each section for review before moving to the next
- Do not draft ahead — partial approval is not full approval

### Section checklist (apply to every section)

- Does this section serve the stated purpose?
- Is the language appropriate for the stated audience?
- Does it connect clearly to the section before and after it?
- Are claims supported? (sources cited, examples given, evidence present)

## Anti-Patterns

- Do NOT draft content before the outline is approved
- Do NOT combine outline and draft in one response
- Do NOT write for a generic audience — always write for the specific reader
- Do NOT skip the three anchors — "just write something" requests still
  require audience and purpose before proceeding
- Do NOT present the entire draft at once for long documents —
  section-by-section review catches problems early

## Document Types and Their Typical Structures

### Academic / Research

Abstract → Introduction → Literature Review → Methodology →
Findings → Discussion → Conclusion → References

### Technical Document

Overview → Prerequisites → Context/Background →
Main Content → Examples → Reference / Appendix

### Research Brief

Purpose → Key Question → Scope → Findings Summary →
Detail → Recommendations → Sources

### Product / User Document

Goal → Audience → Quick Start → Core Content →
Troubleshooting → Reference

### Literary / Creative

Opening hook → Establishment → Development →
Climax/Resolution → Close

## Transition

Once all sections are drafted and approved:
hand off to `plain-language` for a final readability pass.
